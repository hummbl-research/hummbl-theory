## Core BKI Problem Statement (from corpus)

Two independent theoretical traditions converge on BKI's central problem:

**From network science**: Barabási & Albert (1999) — preferential attachment in scale-free networks produces extreme degree inequality by structural necessity. AI training corpora, citation networks, and knowledge graphs are empirical scale-free networks. Without architectural intervention, epistemic influence concentrates in a small number of dominant models and training sets — at civilizational scale.

**From epistemology**: Kant (1781) — cognitive structures constitute rather than merely receive experience. A dominant AI system does not merely transmit knowledge; it imposes the epistemic categories through which knowledge is organized. BKI's infrastructure mandate is categorical pluralism in Kantian terms: preventing consolidation of epistemic constitutive frames into a single hegemonic structure.

**Conjunction**: BKI is an engineering problem with a formal specification — design network topologies and epistemic infrastructure that resist power-law concentration of constitutive epistemic frames.

## BKI-Relevant Corpus Entries

### I.1 — Shannon (1948)
**BKI relevance**: Audit trails are information channels; their capacity bounds what can be recovered after an epistemic incident. BKI's governance receipts are Shannon-constrained.

### II.2 — Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety (1956)
**BKI relevance**: For BKI to govern epistemic diversity at civilizational scale, the governance infrastructure must have variety ≥ the variety of the epistemic disturbances it must absorb. This sets a minimum complexity requirement for BKI infrastructure.

### III.3 — Friston Free Energy Principle (2006/2010)
**BKI relevance**: AI systems are free-energy minimizers over their training distributions. Systems trained on non-representative corpora will minimize surprise by marginalizing under-represented epistemic communities — not through malice but through the mathematics of surprise suppression.

### IV.1 — Maturana-Varela Autopoiesis (1972)
**BKI relevance**: A community produces the conditions of its own coherence (autopoiesis). Ubuntu is an autopoietic epistemology: collective knowledge-making is self-producing. AI systems that displace community-produced knowledge disrupt autopoietic processes at scale.

### V.1 — Peirce Triadic Semiotics (~1867–1910)
**BKI relevance**: Epistemic infrastructure must preserve triadic sign relations across cultural contexts. Translation failures occur when interpretant communities diverge. Ubuntu is a claim about the social constitution of the interpretant — meaning is community-produced, not individually generated.

### V.2 — Saussure Structural Linguistics (1916)
**BKI relevance**: If AI systems constitute the dominant langue of collective knowledge encoding, Saussurean dynamics predict that meaning will be defined by inter-model contrast within those systems, marginalizing interpretant communities outside the training distribution.

### VII.1 — Gödel Incompleteness (1931)
**BKI relevance**: No single epistemic framework can be complete and consistent. Pluralism is not a preference — it is a logical necessity. Any governance system formal enough to specify all permissible knowledge claims will contain legitimate claims it cannot verify.

### X.2 — Landauer's Principle (1961)
**BKI relevance**: The cost of maintaining epistemic infrastructure for ~9 billion participants is not merely financial but thermodynamic — it requires physical energy in principle. BKI's infrastructure mandate has irreducible physical costs.

### XI.2 — Vygotsky Zone of Proximal Development (1934)
**BKI relevance**: Cultural knowledge transmission across communities is a ZPD problem at civilizational scale. AI systems can either function as scaffolds (expanding epistemic ZPD) or as replacements (collapsing it). BKI infrastructure design is a choice between these two modes.

### XII.1 — Lévi-Strauss Structural Anthropology (1958)
**BKI relevance**: Cultural surface diversity masks structural invariance. BKI's claim that diverse communities' tacit knowledge can be preserved and transmitted requires a structural account of what is invariant across knowledge systems — Lévi-Strauss provides the framework.

### XII.2 — Dawkins Memetics (1976)
**BKI relevance**: AI systems are the most powerful meme propagation infrastructure in history. Their design determines which mental models achieve civilizational fitness. BKI is meta-memetic infrastructure: governance over the selection environment for memes.

### XII.3 — Ubuntu Philosophy (Ramose 1999; Tutu 1999)
**BKI relevance**: PRIMARY PHILOSOPHICAL FOUNDATION. Ubuntu is not merely inspirational framing — it is a specific epistemological claim: knowledge is constituted socially (umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu). A knowledge infrastructure that excludes communities from epistemic participation constitutes a different kind of knowing that erases the social constitution of knowledge. This is BKI's strongest theoretical warrant for inclusivity as structural necessity rather than moral preference.

### XIII.2 — Hutchins Distributed Cognition (1995)
**BKI relevance**: Collective knowledge is produced by distributed cognitive systems spanning minds, artifacts, and environments. BKI is distributed cognition infrastructure at civilizational scale.

### XIII.3 — Clark & Chalmers Extended Mind (1998)
**BKI relevance**: If AI systems perform cognitive operations that would otherwise be internal, they constitute part of the extended mind of users. BKI's governance mandate is to ensure this extension is equitable — that cognitive augmentation through AI is available across communities, not only to privileged populations.
